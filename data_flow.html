From ship's bridge to global forecast — tracing the journey of a marine observation

How ship observations travel from sea to science

A ship weather report takes just minutes to travel from the bridge of a vessel in the middle of the Pacific Ocean to a forecast model running in a data centre on the other side of the world. The path it follows — from observation to encoding, transmission, distribution, and archival — is a precisely coordinated international system built up over decades. Understanding this journey helps every participant in the SOT network — from ships' officers to national programme managers — see how their contribution fits into the global picture.

Real-time data path — technical explanation

On board the ship

Observations are recorded by ships' officers at standard synoptic hours (0000, 0600, 1200, 1800 UTC) using electronic logbook software (TurboWin or OBSJMA). The software calculates derived quantities (true wind, mean sea level pressure corrected for bridge height, dew point), applies basic quality checks, and encodes the observation in BBXX format (FM-13 SHIP code). The observation is then transmitted to shore — preferably by email, or via Inmarsat-C satellite using Special Access Code 41 (SAC41) to a Land Earth Station (LES) that accepts BBXX. Automatic Weather Stations (AWS) installed on some ships perform the surface meteorological observation autonomously, typically transmitting hourly.

National collection and GTS distribution

The national meteorological service (NMS) of the receiving country collects BBXX reports — either via its own Land Earth Station, a shared LES, or email — and assembles them into Ship Bulletins. These bulletins are then formatted with the appropriate GTS headers and distributed on the GTS, where they become available to all WMO members globally. The data are assimilated into numerical weather prediction (NWP) models at major forecast centres (ECMWF, NCEP, JMA, and others) — typically within one to two hours of the observation time.

WIS 2.0 transition

As of January 2025, WIS 2.0 is operational and the GTS-to-WIS2 transition phase has begun. WIS 2.0 uses open web standards and a publish-subscribe mechanism to distribute data, replacing the legacy private network architecture of the GTS. SOT data are progressively being published via WIS 2.0 nodes using standardised BUFR encoding (sequence 308014 for VOS ship reports). The GTS will continue operating in parallel during the transition. For guidance on participating in WIS 2.0, see the Learning section.

ASAP and SOOP data flow

ASAP

After a sounding is completed, the ASAP ground station on board the ship encodes the upper-air profile in TEMP SHIP format. The data are transmitted via Inmarsat C to a national collection centre, where they are formatted with appropriate GTS headers and distributed globally. Inmarsat C achieves approximately 99% communication efficiency for ASAP data, ensuring near-complete data recovery. The full chain — from balloon launch to GTS distribution — typically completes within one to two hours.

SOOP / XBT

XBT profiles are logged and processed in real time on shipboard computers provided by the participating national programme (e.g. NOAA AOML). The data are encoded in BATHY format, automatically quality-controlled, and transmitted via satellite on the GTS, typically within 24 hours. Data are simultaneously archived at the Coriolis GDAC and NCEI in near-real time.

Data quality monitoring

VOS data quality is monitored continuously by several major meteorological centres, including ECMWF, Meteo France, and national weather agencies. These centres compare ship reports against NWP model outputs and flag systematic biases, coding errors, and missing reports. Monitoring statistics are distributed to national VOS Program Managers and PMOs, who are expected to investigate flagged ships and take corrective action.

A suite of online VOS quality monitoring tools — maintained by Meteo France — allows Program Managers and PMOs to assess the performance of individual ships and their national fleet in near real time.
