10,000 Ships for the Ocean — the boldest expansion of marine observations in history

The ambition: 10,000 vessels. Real-time data. By 2035.

10,000 ships

targeted by 2035 — a tenfold expansion of the current fleet

500+ ships

already pledged since the UNOC 2025 launch

Supported by WMO, IOC-UNESCO, IMO, and maritime industry pioneers

Today, we use 15 times fewer weather observations over the ocean than over land. That gap is not inevitable — it is a consequence of the practical challenge of instrumenting a vast, sparsely populated environment. But it has real consequences: less accurate marine forecasts, wider uncertainty in storm predictions over the open ocean, and larger unknowns in climate projections for ocean regions.

The maritime industry already operates across every ocean basin, every day. Thousands of commercial vessels transit the very regions where observations are most scarce. The 10,000 Ships for the Ocean initiative asks a simple and transformative question: what if those vessels became part of the global ocean observing system?

The initiative — launched at the United Nations Ocean Conference (UNOC) in Nice in 2025 and supported by WMO, IOC-UNESCO, and IMO — calls on the global shipping industry to equip 10,000 commercial vessels with standardised meteorological and oceanographic sensors by 2035. The data collected will enhance weather forecasting, early warning systems, climate models, and support the development of an AI-powered digital ocean.

Why this matters — the ocean data gap

Vast areas of the global ocean remain poorly monitored. Weather and climate systems that originate over the open ocean are the least well-observed. Tropical cyclones intensify over ocean basins where data are sparse. ENSO patterns develop in regions with fewer in-situ observations than over continents. Climate models carry greater uncertainty in ocean-dominant regions.

At the same time, the global maritime industry — operating fleets of thousands of vessels across all latitudes — represents an extraordinary, underutilised opportunity. Every ship already carries GPS, satellite communications, and a range of environmental monitoring equipment for operational purposes. Adding a standardised suite of meteorological and oceanographic sensors — barometers, anemometers, sea surface temperature sensors, and humidity probes — is not a radical modification. It is a well-tested approach with over 170 years of history under the VOS Scheme.

The 10,000 Ships initiative scales that history to match the ambition of the UN Ocean Decade and the urgency of the climate challenge.

Early commitments — who has already joined

The 10,000 Ships initiative was officially launched in the Blue Zone of UNOC3 in Nice, attracting over 150 participants from international organisations, governments, science institutions, and private sector leaders. Thanks to early commitments from pioneering companies, nearly 500 ships have already been pledged — providing a strong foundation for the long-term public-private partnerships needed to reach the goal.

Early industry signatories include MSC, Maersk, SMT, Höegh Autoliners, Brittany Ferries, and Alcatel Submarine Networks.

Key institutional partners include: IP Paris, Ifremer, IHO, EUMETNET, ECMWF, Mercator Ocean International, BIMCO, and OceansX.

What the initiative will achieve

By transforming commercial vessels into real-time ocean observatories, the 10,000 Ships initiative will close critical observation gaps — especially in under-sampled regions. It directly supports the Global Ocean Observing System (GOOS) and a set of international goals that the world has committed to:

  • EarlyWarnings4All — improving early warning systems for extreme weather events in the most vulnerable communities
  • Global Greenhouse Gas Watch — monitoring greenhouse gas concentrations over the ocean
  • UN Ocean Decade Vision 2030 — particularly Challenge 7, which calls for a major expansion of ship-based observations
  • IMO-WMO objectives on extreme weather events and maritime safety

How it works — and how to join

The initiative is led by OceanOPS — the operational centre of GOOS — and the Ship Observations Team. It operates through a phased approach:

Phase 1 — Pilot and design (2025): Expressions of interest and soft commitments from partners; co-development of the implementation framework; definition of stakeholder roles; mobilisation of instruments, data analysis capabilities, and coordination mechanisms.

Phase 2 — Scale-up (2025–2030): Structured governance model; bilateral engagement with shipping companies worldwide; progressive instrumentation of committed fleets; data integration into GOOS and operational services.

Phase 3 — Full operation (by 2035): 10,000 instrumented vessels providing real-time weather and surface ocean data to the global observing system.

Partners may contribute in ways that align with their capacities and ambitions — by committing vessels, financing instrument procurement and maintenance, or offering in-kind expertise and logistical support. All contributions adhere to international standards for free and unrestricted data exchange.
