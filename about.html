Coordinating global ship-based observations for science, safety and climate

About the Ship Observations Team

The Ship Observations Team (SOT) was established in 2001, jointly by the World Meteorological Organization (WMO) and the Intergovernmental Oceanographic Commission (IOC) of UNESCO. Its founding purpose was to build on the natural synergies between three long-running international programmes that each use ships as ocean and atmospheric observing platforms: the Voluntary Observing Ship (VOS) Scheme, the Automated Shipboard Aerological Programme (ASAP), and the Ship-of-Opportunity Programme (SOOP).

The SOT is a core observing network of the Global Ocean Observing System (GOOS) — the international framework for coordinated, sustained, and high-quality ocean observations. Through GOOS, the SOT contributes to the Essential Ocean Variables (EOVs) that underpin assessments of ocean health, climate variability, and the state of the marine environment. The SOT's ship-based observations feed directly into the GOOS ocean observing architecture, complementing satellite sensors, Argo floats, drifting buoys, and other platforms to provide the in-situ surface and sub-surface measurements that no other observing method can fully substitute.

Vision

Engage the world to lead the global requirements for ship-based data and metadata.

Mission

Provide, and promote the free and unrestricted exchange of, quality ship-based weather and ocean observations for a better understanding and prediction of the marine environment and to support safety of life at sea.

SOT as part of GOOS — contributing to Essential Ocean Variables

The Global Ocean Observing System (GOOS) — co-sponsored by UNESCO-IOC, WMO, United Nation Environment Programme (UNEP), and the International Science Council (ISC) — provides the strategic framework and scientific rationale for global ocean observing. At the heart of this framework is the concept of Essential Ocean Variables (EOVs): the minimum set of measurements needed to characterise the ocean's physical, biogeochemical, and biological state, detect change over time, and support the services that society needs.

The SOT's three programmes contribute to a suite of these EOVs. VOS ships measure sea surface temperature, atmospheric pressure, wind speed and direction, air temperature, humidity, and wave characteristics — all essential physical and atmospheric parameters. XBT SOOP programmes deliver upper ocean temperature profiles — a critical physical EOV that underpins estimates of ocean heat content, and contribute sea surface salinity measurements. ASAP programmes deliver upper-air soundings over the ocean — inputs to the atmospheric observing component of GOOS and the Global Climate Observing System (GCOS).

Together, these observations feed the Global Telecommunication System (GTS) and the new WMO Information System (WIS 2.0), reaching operational centres worldwide within hours of collection — and are then archived for long-term climate science.

Strategic Goals

The SOT Strategy (2022–2027) guides the team's work across four strategic goals:

  1. Global leadership in data and metadata requirements and coordination — The SOT advances the coordination, exchange, and archival of quality ship-based data and metadata. This includes standardising observational and metadata methods, improving quality assurance, promoting the free exchange of observations, raising the technology readiness of shipboard communications, and maintaining and updating technical guidance for the global community.
  2. Expanding the world's ship-based observations — The SOT works to increase the quantity and geographic coverage of ship observations — particularly in data-sparse regions of the global ocean. This means fostering greater national capacity for VOS, ASAP, and XBT SOOP; increasing observations in under-sampled areas; continuing WIGOS implementation activities; and seeking new opportunities for the deployment of measuring devices across commercial, research, and citizen-science fleets.
  3. People and culture — The SOT promotes diverse, inclusive values and fosters strong connections with the global observational community. This includes ensuring regional and gender balance in SOT leadership, maintaining representation in GOOS panels and WMO Technical Commissions, and providing comprehensive training for PMOs, observers, and operators.
  4. Environmental and financial responsibility — The SOT is committed to good stewardship of the world's oceans, sustainable use of resources, and alignment with the strategic plans of WMO, IOC, GCOS, and GOOS.

Membership

SOT membership is open to Member States and Territories of WMO and IOC. Member States participate through their National Meteorological and Hydrological Services (NMHS), which recruit VOS ships, operate ASAP and XBT SOOP programmes, and appoint Port Meteorological Officers (PMOs). Member participation is voluntary, reflecting the collaborative spirit of the international marine observation community.

Today, more than 50 countries contribute actively to one or more SOT programmes. National VOS Focal Points are the primary points of contact for VOS programme matters in each country; their details are maintained in OceanOPS.

Governance

The SOT is governed by the SOT Executive Board (EB), which is led by a Chair and Vice-Chair and is supported by the OceanOPS secretariat. The Executive Board oversees strategic planning and intersessional activities between the biennial SOT session.

Executive Board

The SOT Executive Board (EB) operates under terms of reference established by the SOT at its regular sessions. Key responsibilities of the EB include:

  • Seeking guidance from the SOT at regular sessions on specific issues to be addressed during the intersessional period
  • Acting promptly to address administrative, financial, and planning issues within established guidelines
  • Assisting the Chair with securing the services of a Technical Coordinator
  • Setting working priorities for the Technical Coordinator in alignment with SOT session decisions
  • Conducting a minimum of four teleconferences annually, at least two of which include Task Team Chairs or their representatives
  • Conducting regular biennial team meetings and reporting its activities to the SOT at each session

Task Teams

The SOT operates through a number of Task Teams, each led by a Chair and composed of experts from national meteorological services, research institutions, and partner organizations worldwide.

The SOT Task Team on Recruitment, Promotion and Training oversees VOS recruitment, promotional materials, observer training programmes, and Port Meteorological Officer standards worldwide.

The SOT Task Team on Metadata develops and maintains composite metadata structures for SOT observing networks, aligned with WIGOS metadata standards and WMO Publication No. 47.

The SOT Task Team on Expansion of Independent Class Observations coordinates the expansion of independent class VOS contributions, including citizen science initiatives and projects under the UN Ocean Decade.

The SOT Task Team on Instrument Standards and Satellite Communication Systems provides guidance on instrument testing, standardization, intercalibration, and satellite communication systems for VOS and SOOP.

The SOT Task Team on Key Performance Indicators develops and maintains KPIs for SOT panels covering implementation, data flow, instrumentation, operations, and international cooperation.

The SOT Task Team on VOS Delayed Mode Data maintains and updates the IMMT delayed mode data format and coordinates marine meteorological data exchange in the MCDS.
