The ocean covers more than two-thirds of our planet's surface. Understanding it — and the atmosphere above it — is fundamental to everything from daily weather forecasts to long-term climate science. Yet for most of Earth's history, this vast space was nearly invisible to science. Ships changed that.

For over a century, vessels crossing the world's oceans have been doing more than carrying cargo and passengers: they have been acting as humanity's eyes at sea. The Ship Observations Team (SOT) — a joint programme of the World Meteorological Organization (WMO) and the Intergovernmental Oceanographic Commission (IOC) of UNESCO — coordinates this global effort, bringing together three of the most enduring and impactful marine observation programmes in existence: the Voluntary Observing Ship (VOS) Scheme, the Automated Shipboard Aerological Programme (ASAP), and the eXpendable BathyThermographs (XBTs) Ship-of-Opportunity Programme (SOOP).

Together, these programmes collect the surface meteorological, upper-air, and upper-ocean observations that fuel numerical weather prediction, climate monitoring, marine safety services, and ocean science — reaching parts of the ocean that no satellite or autonomous platform can fully replace.

Three programmes. One mission. Every ocean.

Voluntary Observing Ship (VOS) Scheme

Every day, around 2,000 merchant ships, ferries, and research vessels observe the weather at their location and transmit those observations in near real time to national meteorological services worldwide. The VOS Scheme has been doing this since 1853 — and it remains irreplaceable. Ships measure atmospheric pressure, wind, temperature, humidity, sea surface temperature, waves, and sea ice, providing ground truth that satellites and models cannot replicate.

Automated Shipboard Aerological Programme (ASAP)

The atmosphere doesn't stop at the surface. ASAP stations aboard merchant ships launch weather balloons from the open ocean, collecting vertical profiles of temperature, humidity, and wind from the surface to 30 kilometres altitude — over areas where no land-based radiosonde stations exist. Around 21 ships currently provide these critical upper-air soundings, concentrated in the North Atlantic and expanding globally.

→ Learn about ASAP →

eXpendable BathyThermographs (XBTs) Ship-of-Opportunity Programme (SOOP)

Beneath the surface, the ocean holds answers to some of the most important questions in climate science. XBT SOOP deploys expendable bathythermographs (XBTs) and thermosalinographs (TSGs) from vessels of opportunity — including merchant and research vessels — on regular routes, measuring upper ocean temperature and salinity down to 1,000 metres. Approximately 60 active ships deploy around 19,000 XBTs each year on 36 repeat sampling lines across the world's ocean basins.

Contributing to the safety of all who sail

The International Maritime Organization Circular on Participation in the WMO Voluntary Observing Ships Scheme (IMO MSC Circular 1293) stated that the value of ship-based marine meteorological and oceanographic observations to maritime safety cannot be overstated. For over a century, these reports have provided the real-time feedback weather forecasters need to improve the quality of warnings issued through the Global Maritime Distress and Safety System (GMDSS). Formal investigations into major maritime disasters have underscored how vital these observations are in ensuring the safety of ships, their cargoes, and their crews.

Despite the rise of satellite technology, ship-based data remains essential for "ground-truthing" and providing critical information that satellites cannot easily observe, such as atmospheric pressure and sub-surface ocean measurements. This is especially true in "data-sparse" areas like the polar regions, where a ship's report may be the only data available to forecasters. Even if a vessel’s journey through these regions is temporary, every observation provided is of immense value.

Participation in these WMO/IOC programmes is entirely voluntary. There are no charges incurred by the ship, owner, or operator, as calibrated instruments are supplied and serviced by National Meteorological Services, and transmission costs are typically covered by the meteorological services.

Contribute to ASAP

Does your vessel operate regular ocean crossings? ASAP stations aboard ships launch weather balloons that profile the atmosphere from the surface to 30 km altitude. With equipment installed and maintained at no cost to you, your ship fills a gap that no land-based station can reach. Atmospheric profiles observed have high impact on weather forecasting and marine services.

Participate in SOOP

Does your ship operate on a regular oceanic route? You may be the ideal candidate for hosting an XBT sampling programme. Scientists and technicians handle the technical details — you provide the platform and the route. Help them examining climate changes by deploying XBTs to acquire temperature profiles. These measurements are vital for seasonal weather and climate prediction.

A challenge anyone with a vessel can help solve

There is currently a stark imbalance in global weather observing: we use 15 times fewer data over the ocean than over land to predict the weather worldwide. The consequence is real — marine forecasts are less accurate than land forecasts, storm warnings over the ocean are harder to issue with confidence, and climate models carry larger uncertainties in ocean regions.

Ships already crossing those oceans are the most practical solution we have. Whether your vessel operates a busy trans-Pacific container route, a North Atlantic ferry crossing, or an Antarctic research transect, you are sailing through data-sparse regions where every observation counts.

The SOT provides the infrastructure, the expertise, and the international framework to put your ship's journey to work for science. The ocean asks us to observe it — the SOT shows us how.
