WIS 2.0 — the new era of WMO data sharing, and what it means for ship observations

From the GTS to WIS 2.0 — a once-in-a-generation shift in how weather data moves

WIS 2.0 operational since January 2025

GTS-to-WIS2 transition now underway

Open standards, simpler access, no Member left behind

Since 1971, the Global Telecommunication System (GTS) has been the backbone of WMO's data exchange network — a private, dedicated telecommunication infrastructure that distributes weather observations, forecasts, and warnings among national meteorological services worldwide. For more than five decades, VOS ship reports, ASAP soundings, and SOOP XBT profiles have all been transmitted and distributed through the GTS.

As of January 2025, that system is beginning a fundamental transformation. The WMO Information System 2.0 (WIS 2.0) — approved by the WMO Commission for Observation, Infrastructure and Information Systems (INFCOM) in October 2022 — is now operational. It will progressively replace the GTS with a modern, open, web-based data exchange framework designed for the volume, variety, and velocity of 21st-century earth observation data.

For everyone involved in ship-based observations — national meteorological services, programme managers, PMOs, and shipping companies — understanding WIS 2.0 and how the transition affects SOT data flows is increasingly important.

What is WIS 2.0?

WIS 2.0 is the framework for WMO data sharing in the 21st century. It supports the WMO Unified Data Policy and the Global Basic Observing Network (GBON), and makes international, regional, and national data sharing simple, effective, and cost-efficient.

Three principles define the WIS 2.0 technical framework:

  1. Leveraging open standards. WIS 2.0 uses open, widely adopted web standards — not bespoke WMO systems — reducing costs and lowering the barrier to participation for all Members, particularly developing nations.
  2. Simpler data exchange. WIS 2.0 uses the public Internet as its primary transport network, rather than dedicated private GTS circuits. A publish-subscribe mechanism — similar in concept to messaging applications — allows users to subscribe to data topics of interest and receive new data in real time as it becomes available.
  3. Cloud-ready and scalable. WIS 2.0 is designed to handle the increasing volume and variety of earth observation data, including high-frequency automated observations from tens of thousands of ships, buoys, and sensors.

Three core services of WIS2.0:

Global Broker: Users subscribe to the Global Broker and receive notifications when new data in their chosen topics become available.

Global Cache: A distributed set of servers that temporarily stores freely available core data for rapid retrieval.

Global Discovery Catalogue: A searchable registry of all datasets published on WIS 2.0, providing metadata and access instructions.

What WIS 2.0 means for SOT data

For the Ship Observations Team and its programmes, the WIS 2.0 transition has several practical implications:

  • Data format: VOS ship weather reports (BBXX in FM-13 SHIP code) are progressively being migrated to BUFR binary format (WMO sequence 308014) for distribution on WIS 2.0. National meteorological services will need to encode ship observations in BUFR before publishing to WIS 2.0 nodes. The GTS will continue to carry BBXX in parallel during the transition period.
  • Topic hierarchy: WIS 2.0 organises data by a standardised topic hierarchy rather than GTS bulletin headers. SOT data types (SHIP, TEMP SHIP, BATHY) will be mapped to corresponding WIS 2.0 topics. The SOT Technical Coordinator maintains a reference list of relevant GTS headers and WIS 2.0 topic mappings.
  • Open access: Under the WMO Unified Data Policy, core data — including surface ship observations — is freely and openly accessible to all users through WIS 2.0. This is consistent with the SOT's longstanding commitment to free and unrestricted data exchange.
  • No Member left behind: WIS 2.0 is explicitly designed to reduce the cost and complexity of participation for WMO Members with limited resources. The WMO Secretariat organises regional WIS 2.0 training sessions, and a "WIS 2.0 in a Box" implementation toolkit allows Members to set up WIS 2.0 nodes with minimal infrastructure investment.

Background — a brief history of GTS and WIS

The GTS entered operational life in 1971, providing reliable real-time data exchange for WMO Members. In 2007, the original WMO Information System (WIS) was introduced alongside the GTS, adding a searchable catalogue and global cache to improve data discovery. However, WIS 1.0 only partially met the need for simple, universal access to WMO data.

The growth of the Internet of Things, cloud computing, and open web standards opened the possibility of a fundamentally more capable and accessible successor. WIS 2.0 was designed from the ground up to address the shortfalls of both systems: delivering an increasing volume and variety of real-time data to WMO centres in a reliable, cost-effective, and open way.
